\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

Results are only as useful as the interpretation we bring to them. This chapter steps back from the numbers and asks what they actually mean—for the hypothesis that motivated this work, for practical deployment, and for the honest acknowledgment of what we \emph{cannot} claim.

%% -------------------------------------------------
\section{Complementarity of CEV and IAV Signals}
\label{sec:complementarity}

Our first hypothesis was that CEV and IAV capture different hallucination mechanisms: weak context integration on one side, parametric-memory intrusion on the other. Is this supported by the results?

Yes, to a large extent. For all three backbones, the two branches are close in ranking quality, but are not interchangeable. IAV leads for LLaMA, CEV leads for Qwen, the two are basically tied for Mistral. There is no one signal that is always the best – that is the very case where fusion creates value.

Indeed, the fused probe outperforms both branches in terms of AUROC for \emph{every} backbone (Table~\ref{tab:ragtruth_results}). The gain is small in absolute terms but consistent. This indicates that CEV adds something that IAV fails to capture on its own, and vice versa. They are indeed complementary, not redundant. \textbf{Takeaway:} keeping both probes and fusing their calibrated outputs is the right design choice. collapsing to a single signal would lose reliability on whatever backbone happened to favor the dropped branch.

\section{Safety--Utility Trade-off in Closed-Loop RAG}
\label{sec:safety_utility}

One of the clearest findings from this work is that hallucination mitigation is not free. You can reduce the risk of delivering hallucinated content, but you pay for it in refused queries. The three backbones illustrate three different operating points on this trade-off curve:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2} hits what we consider the sweet spot as it cuts the delivered proxy to 0.1068 (a 67.6\% reduction) while still answering 62\% of queries. Balanced.
    \item \textbf{Qwen3-8B} is the safety-maximizing choice. It achieves the largest reduction (79.3\%) but at a steep cost: it refuses 58\% of the time. If your application cannot tolerate frequent ``I don't know'' responses, Qwen is not the right backbone here.
    \item \textbf{LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct} is the utility-maximizing choice. It keeps 90\% of responses flowing, which is great for user experience, but achieves only a 45.1\% reduction. The residual hallucination proxy remains the highest of the three.
\end{itemize}

There is no particular ``best'' backbone here. The proper one depends entirely on what is more important in a given deployment: limiting risk, optimizing throughput, or something in between. That's a product decision, not a research one.

\section{HaluEval Out-of-Distribution Transfer}
\label{sec:halueval_discussion}

How successfully do probes trained on one benchmark transfer to another? The honest answer is: It depends on the backbone.

LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct generalizes well raw AUROC of \textbf{0.814} on HaluEval, with aligned polarity.  The RAGTruth signal learned translates cleanly. Qwen3-8B does respectably too (\textbf{0.699}), preserving the right label direction.

Mistral is the generic wildcard. More importantly, its adjusted AUROC is actually the highest of the three (0.895). It means that it \emph{separates} HaluEval classes better than anyone but in the incorrect direction. The polarity changes. Our results show that a probe that provides high ratings to hallucinated instances on RAGTruth also assigns high scores to \emph{grounded} examples on HaluEval. Discriminative power, sure. Calibration, not so much.

What does that mean in practice? This means that we can't just train a probe on one distribution and trust it blindly on another. Before deploying in a new domain or with a new prompt format, that we must validate and possibly recalibrate on representative target-domain examples. While this is not a fatal defect of the approach, it is an essential deployment constraint.

\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:limitations}

We want to be clear about the limitations of this effort. We know of the following limits in straightforward terms, because every reader assessing our research needs to know exactly where the boundaries are.

\textbf{First—validation split, not test split} The main AUROC figures are based on a stratified RAGTruth validation split, not the official test set. Published baselines may employ various splits or preprocessing so comparison is a bit apples-to-oranges. We are clear about this distinction throughout yet it is worth emphasizing.

\textbf{Second—probes aren't fact-checkers.} The probes measure hallucination \emph{risk} using activation patterns. They are not prophets. When the internal state of the model looks the same as grounded generation  (see Example~4.4.3 in Section~\ref{sec:illustrative_examples}), the probe will fail to detect a confident but baseless extraction.

\textbf{Third--no usefulness measure.} Even a non-hallucinated response can be imprecise, overcautious, or unhelpful. That aspect of quality is not recorded in our measures.

\textbf{Fourth—transfer is not guaranteed.} The HaluEval polarity inversion on Mistral makes this constraint apparent, not hypothetical. 

 \textbf{Fifth—a threshold controller, not a learnt one.} The deterministic strategy is interpretable and stable, but potentially less flexible than a learnt alternative which is well-trained. We attempted REINFORCE and it didn't function well here (Appendix D), but it doesn't mean a better designed learnt policy couldn't beat our thresholds.

\textbf{Sixth—self-referential metric.} The hallucination reduction percentages (45--79\%) are measured using our own probe as the ground truth. A controller that only delivers low-scoring responses will report low delivered risk by construction. These should be read as reductions in \emph{probe-estimated} risk, not as human-verified improvements.

\textbf{Seventh—single seed, small sample.} The closed-loop evaluation uses 100 SQuAD queries at a nsingle random seed. The RE-RETRIEVE branch was barely exercised. Sampling variance is real, and multi-seed replication on a larger query set is needed to tighten the estimates.

\textbf{Eighth—latency not measured.} Regeneration and re-retrieval obviously cost extra time and tokens. We did not quantify this overhead. Any latency-sensitive deployment would need that analysis.

\textbf{The bottom line:} this system is better understood as an inference-time hallucination-risk control layer, rather than a full factuality oracle. The risk delivered is greatly reduced, but within the bounds of what is noted above.
