\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature_review}

A substantial body of work exists on hallucination detection and mitigation in language models, but these two concerns are rarely addressed within a single unified system. This chapter surveys the relevant literature, organized by method family. We begin with the hallucination phenomenon itself (Section~\ref{sec:lit_hallucination}), then move through black-box and reference-based detectors (Section~\ref{sec:lit_blackbox}), internal-state probing approaches (Section~\ref{sec:lit_probing}), mechanistic work on FFN layers that motivates our IAV signal (Section~\ref{sec:lit_ffn}), closed-loop and self-corrective systems (Section~\ref{sec:lit_closedloop}), and finally cross-architecture generalization studies (Section~\ref{sec:lit_crossarch}).

\section{Hallucination in Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:lit_hallucination}

The term ``hallucination'' in the context of language models refers to output that sounds fluent and confident but is factually unsupported, unverifiable, or outright contradictory to the provided source material. It is not a niche problem—multiple survey papers have identified it as one of the most pressing reliability issues facing modern LLMs~\cite{ji2023hallucination,huang2023survey,rawte2023survey}.

The taxonomy that recurs across these surveys draws a useful distinction. \emph{Intrinsic} hallucination occurs when the generated text directly contradicts the given source—the model says one thing while the evidence says another. \emph{Extrinsic} hallucination is subtler: the model introduces claims that are plausible and might even be true, but that go beyond what the available evidence actually supports. Both types are dangerous in a RAG setting, because the user typically assumes that a retrieval-grounded system is trustworthy.

\section{Black-Box, Statistical, and Reference-Based Detection}
\label{sec:lit_blackbox}

One natural approach to catching hallucinations is to treat the model as a black box and look for inconsistencies in its outputs. SelfCheckGPT~\cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} exemplifies this strategy: it generates multiple responses to the same query and checks whether the factual content stays consistent across samples. The appeal is obvious—no access to model weights or internal states is needed. But there is a catch. If the model is confidently wrong in a systematic way, all sampled responses will contain the same fabrication, and the method will not flag it. Moreover, generating multiple responses per query multiplies inference cost, which is impractical for real-time applications.

Reference-based methods take a different angle. Rather than sampling repeatedly, they compare the generated text against the retrieved evidence directly. RefChecker~\cite{hu2024refchecker}, for instance, decomposes model outputs into fine-grained claim triplets and verifies each against the reference documents. This can be quite effective for post-hoc verification—but it requires a separate verification pipeline, adds latency, and does not leverage the generating model's own internal computation as an intervention signal.

Taken together, these families of methods provide useful baselines and important conceptual contributions. But none of them is quite right for the setting we study here. They either operate \emph{after} generation (too late for closed-loop control), require repeated sampling (too expensive), or depend on external checkers (adding complexity and latency). What we need is a signal that comes from within the generating model itself—something cheap, real-time, and directly actionable.

\section{Internal-State Probing for Hallucination Detection}
\label{sec:lit_probing}

The idea behind internal-state probing is straightforward: if a transformer is producing hallucinated text, maybe its hidden representations already ``know'' something is off—even if the surface text looks fine. Several research groups have explored this intuition.

Azaria and Mitchell~\cite{azaria2023saplma} were among the first to demonstrate this convincingly. They trained simple classifiers on the hidden activations of language models and showed that these classifiers could distinguish true from false statements at roughly \textbf{71--83\% accuracy}, depending on the base model. The implication is clear: internal representations carry truthfulness-relevant information that is not fully captured by output probabilities alone. However, their SAPLMA approach remains purely a detector—it produces a score but does nothing with it. There is no feedback loop connecting that score to any corrective action.

INSIDE (also called EigenScore) pushes the internal-state idea further by analyzing the eigenspectrum of hidden representations across multiple sampled outputs~\cite{chen2024inside}. The covariance structure of these representations can reveal uncertainty that surface-level metrics miss. It is an elegant approach, but it shares the same weakness as SelfCheckGPT: it still requires multiple generations, making it expensive for online use.

MIND~\cite{su2024mind} is particularly relevant to our work because it performs \emph{real-time} hallucination detection using internal states, without needing manual annotation for training. This supports the broader claim that internal representations can be useful during live inference—not just in post-hoc analysis. Still, like most detection-focused methods, MIND does not implement the kind of multi-action controller we develop here.

What emerges from this literature is a consistent finding: internal activations carry useful signals for hallucination detection. Hidden states, attention patterns, FFN activity, uncertainty measures—all of these have proven informative. The missing piece, however, is the connection to action. Detecting hallucination is valuable, but it only becomes \emph{practically} valuable when the detection triggers some change in system behavior. That is the gap our work aims to fill.

\section{FFN Layers as Factual Memory and Motivation for IAV Probing}
\label{sec:lit_ffn}

Why look inside the feed-forward network specifically? The motivation comes from a fascinating line of mechanistic interpretability research.

Geva et al.~\cite{geva2021ffn} demonstrated that transformer FFN layers operate somewhat like key-value memories. The ``keys'' correspond to textual patterns that the input activates, and the ``values'' are the contributions that get written back into the residual stream, influencing what tokens the model will produce next. They also found a depth gradient: lower layers tend to capture surface-level patterns, while upper layers encode more semantic and factual information.

This finding has a direct implication for our work. If FFN layers store and express factual associations, then the intermediate activation \emph{inside} the FFN—before the down-projection collapses it back into the residual stream—should carry a particularly rich signal about what parametric knowledge the model is injecting. That is precisely what our IAV (Intermediate Activation Vector) captures.

By combining this FFN-interior signal (IAV) with the residual-stream signal (CEV), we get two complementary views. CEV tells us about context integration—how well the model has blended the retrieved evidence with the query and the text generated so far. IAV tells us about parametric injection—what the feed-forward network is contributing from its stored knowledge. When these two signals disagree, or when the IAV shows unusual patterns, hallucination may be occurring.

\section{Closed-Loop and Self-Corrective Generation}
\label{sec:lit_closedloop}

The idea of making RAG systems self-corrective is not entirely new, though existing approaches differ substantially from ours in mechanism and scope.

Self-RAG~\cite{asai2024selfrag} trains a language model to generate special ``reflection tokens'' that critique its own output and decide whether additional retrieval is needed. The model learns to retrieve, generate, and self-evaluate all within a single framework. It is an elegant design, but it requires training the model with reflection-token supervision—which means modifying the backbone. Our approach, by contrast, works with completely frozen models and adds the control layer externally.

CRAG~\cite{yan2024crag} takes a different tack. It adds a lightweight evaluator that assesses whether the retrieved documents are actually good enough to answer the query. If not, it triggers corrective retrieval—potentially even web search. CRAG directly addresses the ``bad retrieval'' failure mode, and it does so effectively. But its intervention signal is about \emph{retrieval quality}, not about what the generator is actually doing internally. Our system monitors the generator itself.

Abstention—the ability to simply say ``I don't know''—is another important piece of the puzzle. Wen et al.~\cite{wen2024abstention} survey this capability in LLMs and argue that knowing when to refuse is itself a safety mechanism. We agree, and ABSTAIN is one of the four actions available to our controller.

To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has combined all of the following elements in a single system: hallucination detection from the generating model's own dual CEV/IAV activations, a multi-action inference-time controller (accept/regenerate/re-retrieve/abstain), and operation on completely frozen backbones. Each individual ingredient exists in the literature; the combination and the closed-loop integration are what we contribute.

\section{Cross-Architecture Generalization}
\label{sec:lit_crossarch}

A persistent concern with activation-probing methods is whether they generalize. If you train a probe on one model's hidden states, does the approach transfer to a different model? Or are the learned patterns so architecture-specific that you need to start from scratch each time?

The evidence is mixed. HalluRAG~\cite{frohling2024hallurag} studied internal-state hallucination detection in a closed-domain RAG setting and found that MLP classifiers could reach up to \textbf{75\% test accuracy}—but with limited generalizability across different experimental configurations. This is not surprising given how different models produce very different activation distributions, but it does underscore the need to test on more than one backbone.

CLAP (Cross-Layer Attention Probing)~\cite{zhao2024clap} goes further by evaluating fine-grained hallucination detection across \textbf{five LLMs and three task types}. Their results confirm that activation-based signals do transfer to some degree, but the strength and reliability of that transfer varies considerably across models.

Our evaluation is designed with this concern front and center. We test the identical CEV/IAV probing framework and controller design on three decoder-only transformers—Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct, and Qwen3-8B—which differ in their tokenizers, training data, layer counts, and FFN intermediate dimensions. The framework stays the same; the probe weights and thresholds are calibrated per backbone. This gives us a controlled test of reusability: the architecture is shared, but each deployment requires backbone-specific training. That turns out to be an honest and practical characterization of where internal-state probing stands today.
