\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature_review}

There exists a substantial amount of work on hallucination detection and mitigation in language models, but these two concerns are seldom addressed in a single unified system. This chapter reviews the literature relevant to each method family. We first discuss the phenomenon of hallucination itself (Section~\ref{sec:lit_hallucination}), before covering black-box and reference-based detectors (Section~\ref{sec:lit_blackbox}), internal-state probing approaches (Section~\ref{sec:lit_probing}), mechanistic work on FFN layers which motivates our IAV signal (Section~\ref{sec:lit_ffn}), closed-loop and self-corrective systems (Section~\ref{sec:lit_closedloop}), and finally cross-architecture generalization studies (Section~\ref{sec:lit_crossarch}).

\section{Hallucination in Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:lit_hallucination}

The term ``hallucination'' in the context of language models refers to output that sounds fluent and confident but is factually unsupported, unverifiable, or outright contradictory to the provided source material. It is not a niche problem, having been identified as one of the most pressing reliability issues for modern LLMs in multiple survey papers~\cite{ji2023hallucination,huang2023survey,rawte2023survey}. In RAG especially, entity-based knowledge conflicts between evidence retrieved and parametric memory can lead to hallucination even when the retriever provides relevant passages~\cite{longpre2021entity}.

The taxonomy that is repeated across these surveys makes a useful distinction. \emph{Intrinsic} hallucination is when the generated text directly contradicts the given source, the model says one thing, but the evidence says another. \emph{Extrinsic} hallucination is more subtle: the model adds claims that are plausible and might even be true, but that go beyond what the available evidence actually supports. Both types are dangerous in a RAG setting, as the user usually assumes that a retrieval-grounded system is trustworthy.

\section{Black-Box, Statistical, and Reference-Based Detection}
\label{sec:lit_blackbox}

A natural way to catch hallucinations is to regard the model as a black box and check for discrepancies in the output. One example of such approach is SelfCheckGPT~\cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} which creates several replies for the same question and checks the consistency of the factual information across samples. The appeal is clear: you don’t need access to model weights or internal states. But here is the catch. If the model is consistently incorrect with high confidence, then all sampled replies will have the same fabrication and the approach will fail to detect it. Additionally, the inference cost is paid for by generating numerous responses for each query, which is not applicable for real-time applications.

Another one is reference-based approaches. Rather than sampling many times, they compare the created text directly with the retrieved evidence. For example, RefChecker~\cite{hu2024refchecker} decomposes the model output into fine-grained claim triplets, then validates each triplet with the reference documents. This can be quite successful for post hoc verification - but requires a separate verification pipeline, increases latency and does not leverage the generating model’s own internal computation as an intervention signal.

These groups of approaches together give helpful baselines and major conceptual contributions. But none of them is completely suited for the context we analyze here. They either need frequent sampling (costly), or work \emph{after} generation (late for closed-loop control), or rely on external checkers (complexity and latency). What we want is a signal from the producing model itself, something cheap, real time and actionable.

\section{Internal-State Probing for Hallucination Detection}
\label{sec:lit_probing}

The concept behind internal-state probing is simple: if a transformer is generating hallucinated text, perhaps its deep representations already “know” something is amiss, even if the surface text looks fine. This notion has been pursued by several groups of researchers.

Azaria and Mitchell~\cite{azaria2023saplma} were amongst the first to convincingly show this.  They trained simple classifiers on the hidden activations of language models, and demonstrated that these classifiers could discriminate true from incorrect claims at about \textbf{71--83\% accuracy} depending on the base model. The inference is obvious: internal representations contain information about veracity not completely represented in output probabilities. But their SAPLMA method is a pure detector, producing a score but not doing anything with it. That score does not feed back into any corrective action.

INSIDE (also called EigenScore) extends the internal-state idea by examining the eigenspectrum of hidden representations over multiple sampled outputs~\cite{chen2024inside}. Surface-level metrics miss uncertainty that can be revealed by the covariance structure of these representations. That's a good method, but it shares the same weakness as SelfCheckGPT: it still needs multiple generations which is expensive for online use.

MIND~\cite{su2024mind} is most related to our work, which performs \emph{real-time} hallucination detection based on internal states without manual annotation for training. This supports the more general claim that internal representations can be useful at live inference, not just at post-hoc analysis. However, as most detection-based methods, MIND does not employ the multi-action controller we develop here.

A consistent finding in the literature is that useful signals for hallucination detection can be found in the internal activations. Hidden states, attention patterns, FFN activity, uncertainty measures, all of them have provided useful information. But the missing piece is the connection to action. Detection of hallucination is useful, but it becomes \emph{practically} useful only if detection causes some change in system behavior. That’s the gap we’re trying to close.

\section{FFN Layers as Factual Memory and Motivation for IAV Probing}
\label{sec:lit_ffn}

Why look inside the feed-forward network specifically? The motivation comes from a fascinating line of mechanistic interpretability research.

According to Geva et al.~\cite{geva2021ffn}, Transformer FFN layers are similar to key-value memories. The “ keys ” are textual patterns that trigger the input . The “ values ” are the contributions that are written back into the residual stream , which determines what tokens the model will output next . They also noticed a depth gradient with lower layers encoding surface patterns and upper layers encoding more semantic and factual information. Further empirical work has examined how these stored associations can be updated and modified~\cite{qiu2024updating}.

This has a direct consequence on our work. If FFN layers store and express factual associations, then the intermediate activation \emph{inside} the FFN, before the down-projection collapses it back into the residual stream, should contain a particularly rich signal about what parametric knowledge the model is injecting. This is exactly what our IAV (Intermediate Activation Vector) captures.

This gives us two complementary views: the residual-stream signal (CEV) and the FFN-interior signal (IAV). CEV discusses context integration, how well the model has integrated the retrieved evidence with the query and the text generated so far. IAV tells us about parametric injection - what the feed-forward network is contributing from its stored knowledge. Hallucination is possible if these two signals are not in agreement or if the IAV displays abnormal patterns.

\section{Closed-Loop and Self-Corrective Generation}
\label{sec:lit_closedloop}

The idea of making RAG systems self-corrective is not entirely new, though existing approaches differ substantially from ours in mechanism and scope.

Self-RAG~\cite{asai2024selfrag} trains a language model to generate special ``reflection tokens'' that critique its own output and decide whether additional retrieval is needed. The model learns to retrieve, generate, and self-evaluate all within a single framework. It is an elegant design, but it requires training the model with reflection-token supervision, which means modifying the backbone. Our approach, by contrast, works with completely frozen models and adds the control layer externally.

CRAG~\cite{yan2024crag} takes a different path. It adds a lightweight evaluator that checks if the obtained documents are good enough to answer the query. If not, it triggers corrective retrieval, possibly even a web search. CRAG explicitly handles the “bad retrieval” failure mode, and does so well. But its intervention signal is about \emph{quality of retrieval}, not about what the generator is actually doing internally. “Our system watches the generator itself.

Abstention is another crucial piece of the jigsaw, the ability to just say ``I don't know.'' Wen et al. \cite{wen2024abstention} survey this capacity in LLMs and argue that knowing when to reject is a safety strategy in itself. We agree, and ABSTAIN is one of four actions open to our controller.

To our knowledge, no previous work has integrated all of the following in a single system, namely, hallucination detection from the generating model's own dual CEV/IAV activations, a multi-action inference-time controller (accept/regenerate/re-retrieve/abstain), and operation on entirely frozen backbones. All the individual requisites are in the literature;  the combination and the closed-loop integration are what we contribute.

\section{Cross-Architecture Generalization}
\label{sec:lit_crossarch}

A concern with activation-probing methods is whether they generalise. Does the method transfer to a different model if we train a probe on one model's hidden states? Or are the learned patterns so specific to the architecture that we have to start over each time?

The evidence is mixed. HalluRAG~\cite{frohling2024hallurag} studied internal-state hallucination detection in a closed-domain RAG setting and found that MLP classifiers could reach up to \textbf{75\% test accuracy} but with limited generalizability across different experimental configurations. This is not surprising given how different models produce very different activation distributions, but it does underscore the need to test on more than one backbone.

CLAP (Cross-Layer Attention Probing)~\cite{zhao2024clap} goes further by evaluating fine-grained hallucination detection across \textbf{five LLMs and three task types}. Their results confirm that activation-based signals do transfer to some degree, but the strength and reliability of that transfer varies considerably across models.

Our evaluation is designed with this concern front and center. We test the identical CEV/IAV probing framework and controller design on three decoder-only transformers—Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct, and Qwen3-8B which differ in their tokenizers, training data, layer counts, and FFN intermediate dimensions. The framework remains the same, i.e., the probe weights and thresholds are calibrated per backbone. This gives us a controlled reusability test: the architecture is shared, but each deployment requires backbone-specific training. This is an honest and practical characterization of the state of internal state probing at current.
