\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{ch:conclusion}

This work introduced a closed-loop Retrieval-Augmented Generation framework that connects internal-state hallucination detection with inference-time correction. Rather than using a detector only to score generated responses after the fact, the proposed system feeds the calibrated hallucination-risk estimate back into the RAG loop through a deterministic controller that can accept, regenerate, re-retrieve, or abstain.

The system instruments frozen decoder-only language models with PyTorch forward hooks and extracts two complementary activation signals: \textbf{Contextualized Embedding Vectors (CEV)} from the residual stream and \textbf{Intermediate Activation Vectors (IAV)} from the SwiGLU feed-forward sublayer. Separate lightweight MLP probes are trained on RAGTruth hallucination labels and fused into a calibrated hallucination probability that drives the closed-loop controller.

Across three open-weight decoder backbones, the fused CEV/IAV probe achieves strong RAGTruth validation performance: \textbf{0.8596 AUROC} for Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, \textbf{0.8808 AUROC} for Qwen3-8B, and \textbf{0.8689 AUROC} for LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct. These results surpass all published and reproduced baseline detectors considered in this study, while being the only method with closed-loop control capability.

The closed-loop evaluation demonstrates that internal hallucination scores can be used not only for detection but also for controlling system behavior: the controller reduces delivered hallucination risk across all three backbones, with the per-backbone safety--utility trade-off summarized in Table~\ref{tab:cross_model} and Section~\ref{sec:safety_utility}.

The HaluEval analysis further shows that probe transfer is distribution-dependent. Qwen3-8B and LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct preserve aligned out-of-domain discrimination, while Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 shows strong polarity inversion. This finding highlights the need for target-domain validation or recalibration before deployment in new settings.

\textbf{Future work} should prioritize evaluation on the official RAGTruth test split, multi-seed replication across all backbones, stronger target-domain calibration, content-quality checks for omission and relevance failures, and extension to non-SwiGLU architectures.

\section{Architecture-Agnostic Scope}
\label{sec:agnostic_scope}

The proposed framework is \textbf{architecture-agnostic in design}, but \textbf{not weight-transfer agnostic}.

It is architecture-agnostic because the same overall procedure is used across all evaluated backbones:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Register hooks at proportional decoder depths.
    \item Extract CEV and IAV features.
    \item Train CEV and IAV MLP probes.
    \item Calibrate and fuse probe probabilities.
    \item Feed the fused hallucination-risk score into the same closed-loop controller.
\end{enumerate}

However, the trained probe weights do not transfer directly across backbones. Each model has its own activation geometry, hidden-state scale, tokenizer behavior, and FFN intermediate dimension. Therefore, the framework is reusable, but each deployment requires backbone-specific probe training and threshold calibration.

\begin{table}[!htb]
\centering
\caption{What stays fixed and what changes across models.}

\begin{tabular}{@{}lcp{7cm}@{}}

\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Agnostic?} & \textbf{Explanation} \\

\end{tabular}

\textbf{Final agnosticity statement:} The system is architecturally reusable across related decoder-only transformer families, but each backbone requires its own probe weights and calibration thresholds.
