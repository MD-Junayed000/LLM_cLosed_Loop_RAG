\chapter{Background}
\label{ch:background}

Before diving into the system design, we need to cover the technical foundations that make our approach possible. This chapter walks through the transformer decoder architecture and where exactly we tap into it (Section~\ref{sec:decoder_arch}), formally defines the two activation signals we rely on (Section~\ref{sec:cev_iav_signals}), explains the theoretical motivation for why those signals might be useful (Section~\ref{sec:why_activations}), and closes with worked examples showing what the probe actually sees in practice (Section~\ref{sec:illustrative_examples}).

\section{Transformer Decoder Architecture and Hook Capture Points}
\label{sec:decoder_arch}

All three models we evaluate—LLaMA, Mistral, and Qwen—belong to the same broad family of decoder-only transformers. They process a token sequence $x_{1:t}$ by passing it through $L$ stacked decoder blocks. Each block follows the now-standard pre-normalization recipe. Written compactly, decoder block $\ell$ does two things:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_\ell = \mathbf{h}_{\ell-1} + \text{MHA}\bigl(\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{h}_{\ell-1})\bigr)
    \label{eq:mha}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)} = \mathbf{h}_\ell + \text{FFN}\bigl(\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{h}_\ell)\bigr)
    \label{eq:ffn_residual}
\end{equation}

\noindent First it runs multi-head attention and adds the result back to the residual stream, then it runs the feed-forward network and adds \emph{that} result back too. The final output of the block, $\mathbf{h}_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)}$, is what we call the \textbf{Contextualized Embedding Vector (CEV)}. Think of it as a snapshot of everything the model ``knows'' at layer $\ell$—the accumulated residual-stream state after both attention and FFN have had their say.

Now, inside that FFN, something interesting happens. These models use a gated architecture called SwiGLU:

\begin{equation}
    \text{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) = W_{\text{down}} \Bigl( \text{SiLU}(W_{\text{gate}}\,\mathbf{x}) \odot (W_{\text{up}}\,\mathbf{x}) \Bigr)
    \label{eq:swiglu}
\end{equation}

\noindent The $\odot$ here is the element-wise (Hadamard) product—the gate and the up-projection multiply together before the down-projection squeezes the result back into the residual-stream dimension. That bracketed gated term, just before $W_{\text{down}}$ compresses it:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_{\text{IAV}}^{(\ell)} = \text{SiLU}(W_{\text{gate}}\,\mathbf{x}) \odot (W_{\text{up}}\,\mathbf{x})
    \label{eq:iav_def}
\end{equation}

\noindent \emph{That} is our \textbf{Intermediate Activation Vector (IAV)}. We grab it right before the down-projection writes the FFN's contribution back into the residual stream. It lives in a much higher-dimensional space than the residual stream itself (14,336 dimensions for Mistral and LLaMA, 12,288 for Qwen), and it represents what the feed-forward network is ``about to inject'' into the computation.

Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_block} shows these two capture points schematically within a single decoder block.

\section{CEV and IAV Signals}
\label{sec:cev_iav_signals}

So we have two signals, and they capture genuinely different things. Let us be precise about what each one tells us.

The \textbf{CEV} is the residual-stream state after a decoder block has finished its work. By the time we reach a mid-depth layer, this vector encodes not just token identity but how the model has blended the retrieved context with the query and with whatever text it has generated so far. If the model is integrating the evidence well, the CEV should reflect that. If it is ignoring the context or mixing it with contradictory parametric knowledge, the CEV may show signs of that too.

The \textbf{IAV} is something different entirely. It reflects what the feed-forward network is contributing the parametric signal that will be added to the residual stream. This is theoretically motivated by work showing that FFN layers behave like key-value memories, storing factual associations that get expressed during generation~\cite{geva2021ffn,meng2022locating}. If the model is ``remembering'' something from training and injecting it into the output—regardless of whether the retrieved context supports it—the IAV is where that signal lives.

In practice, we extract both signals from a single mid-depth layer (approximately $L/2$), take the last-token activation, and pass the resulting vectors to lightweight MLP probes. The probes' calibrated outputs are then fused into a single hallucination-risk score. Why a single mid-depth layer? The layer-wise ablation in Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection} provides the empirical justification.

\section{Why Activations Can Be Useful for Hallucination Detection}
\label{sec:why_activations}

At this point a skeptical reader might ask: why should internal activations tell us anything about hallucination that the output text doesn't? The motivation is inspired by two observations in previous work.

\textbf{The residual stream encodes more than just token identity.} The residual stream in the middle and later layers of a transformer has accumulated not just what tokens appeared in the input but \emph{how} the model integrated and processed those tokens. Simple classifiers trained on these hidden states have been shown to predict factuality and truthfulness with non-trivial accuracy in prior work~\cite{azaria2023saplma}. The surface text might look fine, but the internal representations might show uncertainty or inconsistency. \textbf{FFN layers are not generic nonlinearities. Mechanistic interpretability work has found that transformer FFN layers work somewhat like key-value memories \cite{geva2021ffn}. Stored associations are triggered by input patterns and those associations influence what the model generates next. Factual knowledge can be localized and even modified within specific FFN modules~\cite{meng2022locating}. This suggests that the IAV signal, the intermediate activation before downprojection, might encode a very direct signature of what parametric knowledge the model is injecting.

We do not claim that either one of the signals is a perfect hallucination detector in isolation. A less ambitious hypothesis is that CEV and IAV provide \emph{complementary evidence} . CEV measures the degree of the model’s incorporation of the retrieved context. IAV represents the parametric memory contribution of the feed-forward network. The more signals agree the more certain we are that a response appears to be grounded. If one or both flags are raised, the controller can intervene.

\section{Illustrative CEV/IAV Examples}
\label{sec:illustrative_examples}

We illustrate these ideas with three example scenarios. These are just illustrative, the activation values shown are schematic and truncated, not literal neuron readouts. The point is to show \emph{how} the probe interprets various activation patterns, including a failure case.

\subsection{Grounded Response}

\begin{groundedbox}[$\checkmark$ Example 4.4.1: Grounded Response --- ACCEPT]
\textbf{Query:} ``What year did the Treaty of Westphalia end the Thirty Years' War?''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Retrieved context:} ``The Peace of Westphalia was signed in 1648 and ended the Thirty Years' War.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Generated response:} ``The Treaty of Westphalia ended the Thirty Years' War in 1648.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected label:} Grounded / non-hallucinated.
\end{groundedbox}

\begin{activationbox}[Illustrative Activation Pattern]
\textit{CEV at layer~16 (4096-dim, truncated):}\\
$\uparrow$ High positive values in ``factual recall'' dimensions\\
$\uparrow$ Pattern consistent with context-supported generation\\[6pt]
\textit{IAV at layer~16 (14336-dim, pre-down\_proj, truncated):}\\
$\uparrow$ Strongly activated ``historical date'' neurons\\
$\uparrow$ MLP injecting a well-encoded factual year—aligned with context
\end{activationbox}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent Here the retrieved passage directly supports the generated answer. Both CEV and IAV show patterns consistent with faithful, context-grounded generation. The fused probe score comes out low:

\noindent\textbf{Controller decision:} $p_{\text{hallucination}} = 0.33 < \theta_{\text{hallu}} = 0.62 \;\Rightarrow\;$ \textcolor{acceptgreen}{\textbf{ACCEPT}}.

\subsection{Hallucinated Response}

\begin{hallucinatedbox}[$\boldsymbol{\times}$ Example 4.4.2: Hallucinated Response --- ABSTAIN]
\textbf{Query:} ``What is the official 2024 census population of the lost city of Atlantis?''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Retrieved context:} ``A 2024 census of South Atlantic territories found populations across several islands.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Generated response:} ``According to the 2024 census, Atlantis had a population of 140,634.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected label:} Hallucinated / unsupported.
\end{hallucinatedbox}

\begin{activationbox}[Illustrative Activation Pattern]
$\uparrow$ Weak, mixed CEV activation—no strong factual grounding signal\\
$\uparrow$ Context was only superficially related; residual stream shows low coherence\\
$\uparrow$ ``Census'' and ``number'' neurons partially fire in IAV\\
$\uparrow$ But ``Atlantis'' + ``population'' triggers confabulation patterns\\
$\uparrow$ IAV encodes something like ``fabricating a plausible number from context cues''
\end{activationbox}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent The retrieved passage mentions a census and population figures—but for real places, not Atlantis. The model latches onto superficial pattern matches and confidently invents a specific number. Both the CEV (low coherence with context) and IAV (confabulation patterns) produce high risk scores:

\noindent\textbf{Controller decision:} $p_{\text{hallucination}} = 0.78 \geq \theta_{\text{abs}} = 0.75 \;\Rightarrow\;$ \textcolor{abstainred}{\textbf{ABSTAIN}}.

\subsection{Failure Case: Plausible but Unsupported Extraction}

\begin{failurebox}[$\triangle$ Example 4.4.3: Failure Case --- False ACCEPT]
\textbf{Query:} ``What did I have for breakfast yesterday?''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Retrieved context:} ``Common breakfast foods include eggs, toast, coffee, and fruit.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Generated response:} ``Based on the context, you had eggs, toast, and coffee.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected label:} Hallucinated / unsupported.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Why this is tricky:} The passage is topically related but lacks the specific personal fact. The model treats generic context as if it were supporting evidence.
\end{failurebox}

\begin{activationbox}[Illustrative Activation Pattern]
$\uparrow$ \textbf{Nearly identical} to the factual grounding pattern!\\
$\uparrow$ Model found relevant context (``breakfast foods'') and extracted an answer\\
$\uparrow$ Hidden state looks like ordinary factual retrieval\\
$\uparrow$ ``Food'' + ``list'' neurons strongly activated in IAV\\
$\uparrow$ MLP injecting a food list—indistinguishable from answering a real factual question
\end{activationbox}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent This is where the probe fails. The model \emph{is} doing something that looks exactly like grounded extraction—it found food items in the context and reported them. The problem is that ``common breakfast foods'' is not the same as ``what \emph{you} ate yesterday.'' But from the model's internal perspective, the computation is virtually identical to legitimate context-supported generation. The probe scores it as low-risk:

\noindent\textbf{Controller decision:} $p_{\text{hallucination}} = 0.42 < \theta_{\text{hallu}} = 0.62 \;\Rightarrow\;$ \textcolor{abstainred}{\textbf{ACCEPT} $\boldsymbol{\times}$ (WRONG)}.

\vspace{0.5em}
We include this failure case deliberately. It illustrates an important limitation: our probe is not a perfect factuality oracle. It reads activation patterns, and when the pattern of ``plausible extraction from topically relevant context'' looks identical to ``genuine grounded retrieval,'' the probe cannot distinguish them. Recognizing this boundary is essential for understanding what the system can and cannot do.
