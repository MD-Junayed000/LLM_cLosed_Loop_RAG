\chapter{Failure Analysis}
\label{ch:failure_analysis}

This chapter analyzes weaker design choices and explains why the final configuration was chosen. Section~\ref{sec:failed_choices} discusses failed or weaker design choices. Section~\ref{sec:why_best} explains why the final configuration works best. Section~\ref{sec:backbone_strengths} presents backbone-level strengths and limitations, and Section~\ref{sec:alternative_methods} discusses why some alternative approaches were insufficient.

\section{Failed or Weaker Design Choices}
\label{sec:failed_choices}

\subsection{CEV-Only Probing}

A CEV-only probe was evaluated as a residual-stream baseline. CEV-only probing matches the approach used in earlier hidden-state probing methods. The limitation is conceptual: when hallucination arises from parametric-memory intrusion, the relevant signal may appear most clearly inside the FFN before it is projected back into the residual stream.

\textbf{Verdict:} CEV-only probing is useful but incomplete. It should remain as an ablation baseline.

\subsection{IAV-Only Probing}

IAV-only probing is the strongest single branch in the ablation results. This confirms that FFN intermediate activations carry substantial hallucination-relevant signal. However, some hallucinations arise from weak attention to retrieved evidence or poor context integration, which are better reflected in CEV.

\textbf{Verdict:} IAV is the strongest individual signal, but fusion is more robust.

\subsection{Unconstrained Fusion Search}

An unconstrained fusion search can overfit to the stronger-looking branch on a particular validation fold, losing complementarity.

\textbf{Verdict:} Fusion should be constrained to avoid collapse into single-signal behavior.

\subsection{Mean Pooling Across All Tokens}

Mean pooling over all generated tokens dilutes the hallucination-relevant signal that appears near the final decision state.

\textbf{Verdict:} Last-token pooling is more appropriate for autoregressive decoder probing.

\subsection{Three-Depth (Triple-Layer) Concatenation}

Concatenating features from three proportional depths ($L/4$, $L/2$, $3L/4$) was the original design. In practice it was retired after the controlled layer-wise ablation in Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection}, which shows the three-depth concatenation never wins.

\textbf{Verdict:} A single mid-depth ($L/2$) hook is the better choice and is adopted in the final system.

\subsection{Hard Labels Without Label Smoothing}

Training with hard one-hot labels makes the probe overconfident, which harms calibration. Label smoothing ($\varepsilon = 0.05$) reduces this overconfidence.

\textbf{Verdict:} Label smoothing is useful because the controller needs calibrated probabilities.

\subsection{Learned REINFORCE Controller}

A learned REINFORCE-style policy controller was attempted but not retained. The rollout setting did not provide a stable enough reward signal.

\textbf{Verdict:} The deterministic controller is preferable for the current study. Learned policies should be revisited with larger rollout budgets.

\section{Why the Final Configuration Works Best}
\label{sec:why_best}

\begin{table}[!htb]
\centering
\caption{Final design choices and the failures they address.}
\label{tab:design_choices}
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{5.5cm} p{8cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Final Design Choice} & \textbf{Failure Prevented} \\
\midrule
Dual CEV + IAV probing & Avoids missing either context-integration or FFN-memory signals \\
Single mid-depth ($L/2$) hook & Best AUROC per parameter; avoids triple's ${\sim}3\times$ cost \\
Last-token extraction & Preserves the final autoregressive decision state \\
Separate CEV and IAV probes & Avoids forcing different vector spaces into one classifier \\
Temperature calibration & Converts raw logits into usable probabilities \\
Validation-tuned fusion & Combines signals while controlling over-reliance \\
Label smoothing & Reduces overconfidence under noisy labels \\
Backbone-specific thresholds & Accounts for different activation distributions \\
Deterministic controller & Avoids unstable learned policy under sparse rewards \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The final architecture works because each design choice addresses a specific empirical or theoretical weakness observed in earlier variants.

\section{Why Some Alternative Approaches Were Insufficient}
\label{sec:alternative_methods}

\textbf{SelfCheckGPT-style consistency methods} require multiple sampled generations and can fail when the model consistently repeats the same incorrect answer. They measure sampling instability, not necessarily factual faithfulness to retrieved evidence.

\textbf{External judge models} can provide strong verification, but they add cost, latency, and dependency on a separate model. This work instead focuses on using the generating model's own internal signals.

\textbf{Retrieval improvement alone} is also insufficient. Better retrieval can help when the retrieved evidence is weak, but it cannot fix cases where the model ignores good evidence or fabricates despite relevant context.

\textbf{Alternative-method verdict:} Retrieval improvement, black-box consistency, and external judging are useful but incomplete. The proposed system targets a different design point: lightweight, single-pass, internal-signal-driven hallucination control at inference time.
