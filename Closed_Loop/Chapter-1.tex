\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

This opening chapter lays the groundwork for the research presented in this thesis. We begin by motivating the problem of why hallucination in Retrieval-Augmented Generation remains an unsolved reliability challenge, explain the mechanism that makes our internal-state approach feasible: PyTorch forward hooks, and then formalize the research framework including the problem statement, objectives, technical challenges, contributions, and evaluation scope.

\section{The Hallucination Problem in RAG}
\label{sec:hallucination_problem}

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{Images/Figure1.png}
    \caption[Conceptual comparison between standard open-loop RAG and the proposed closed-loop RAG pipeline.]{Conceptual comparison between standard open-loop RAG and the proposed closed-loop RAG pipeline. In standard RAG, the generated answer is returned without an internal reliability check. In the proposed system, internal activation probes estimate hallucination risk and route the answer through a controller.}
    \label{fig:open_vs_closed}
\end{figure}

In a typical RAG system: relevant passages are retrieved, a language model generates an answer conditioned on those passages, and the answer goes straight to the user. Nobody and no mechanism within the pipeline checks whether what was generated actually matches the retrieved evidence. Figure~\ref{fig:open_vs_closed} contrasts this open-loop design with the closed-loop alternative we propose.

The problem is subtle. Even when the retriever supplies perfectly relevant documents, the model can still fabricate information. It might rely on parametric knowledge that contradicts the evidence, misread the passage, or only partially integrate the context. The result is a fluent, plausible-sounding answer that nonetheless contains unsupported claims. We call this the \textbf{open-loop failure mode} hallucinated content can reach the user because nothing in the pipeline acts on the reliability of the output before delivery.

Our proposed pipeline introduces a feedback stage. While the model generates its response, forward hooks silently copy activation tensors from within the decoder. Lightweight probes then convert those tensors into an estimate of hallucination risk. A controller receives this estimate and makes an operational decision: accept the answer, regenerate it, fetch better evidence, or abstain entirely. The probability illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:open_vs_closed} is meant to show the \emph{kind} of signal the controller works with, not a fixed universal threshold.

In essence, this study aims to \emph{close the gap between detecting hallucination and actually doing something about it}. Rather than merely scoring a response after the fact, the system feeds that score back into the generation loop so that unreliable answers never reach the end user.

\section{Why Hooks?}
\label{sec:why_hooks}

Before describing the full closed-loop system, it is worth explaining the mechanism that makes internal-state probing practical. The short answer is \textbf{PyTorch forward hooks}, lightweight callback functions that let us observe intermediate tensors during the forward pass of the model, without changing anything about the computation.

\subsection{What Is a Forward Hook?}
\label{subsec:forward_hook}

Basically, a forward hook is a function that gets called whenever a particular module of a neural-network runs. When triggered the hook receives what the module produced (or consumed) and can save a copy for later analysis. Importantly, the hook does not interfere with the forward pass, it just observes.

Hooks in our setting are added \emph{passively}. They store separate copies of activation tensors during inference, and do not change the model’s output, decoding strategy, or learned parameters. This passive observation is what makes the approach non-intrusive: the language model continues to execute exactly as it would without the hooks.

Why is this important?? Because it means we can inspect what the model is ``thinking'' internally at any layer, for any sublayer computation—while it processes retrieved context and produces its answer. We are not limited to the final generated text or the output token probabilities; we can look deeper.

The copied activation is passed to a lightweight probe, which estimates hallucination risk for the closed-loop controller.}

\subsection{Why Hooks Are Needed in This System}
\label{subsec:hooks_needed}

Our system relies on two complementary internal signals, each capturing a different aspect of the model's computation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Contextualized Embedding Vector (CEV):} This is the residual-stream output of a decoder block—essentially the accumulated state after the block has performed both attention and feed-forward processing. It tells us something about how well the model has integrated the retrieved context with the query and the text generated so far.
    \item \textbf{Intermediate Activation Vector (IAV):} This is the SwiGLU feed-forward intermediate—the gated activation computed \emph{inside} the FFN sublayer, just before the final down-projection writes it back into the residual stream. It captures the feed-forward network's own contribution, which prior work has linked to parametric-memory retrieval.
\end{enumerate}

Now, the CEV is relatively easy to obtain. Most transformer implementations can return all hidden states on request. But the IAV—the intermediate value inside the FFN before the down-projection is not something standard APIs expose. You would need to rewrite the model's forward method or, much more cleanly, attach a hook to the right submodule. Forward hooks are therefore \emph{essential} for capturing the IAV signal without modifying the backbone code.

In practice, we register a single hook at one mid-depth decoder layer (approximately $\ell^* = \lfloor L/2 \rfloor$). Both the CEV and IAV from that layer are extracted, scored by lightweight MLP probes, and the resulting hallucination-risk estimate is passed to the controller. The choice of a single mid-depth layer rather than concatenating features from multiple depths—is justified empirically through the layer-wise ablation reported in Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection}.

\subsection{Why Not Just Read Hidden States After Inference?}
\label{subsec:why_not_after}

A reasonable question. Standard HuggingFace APIs do let you retrieve all residual-stream hidden states with a single flag. But they do \emph{not} expose FFN intermediate activations the IAV signa without custom code. The gated intermediate inside the SwiGLU computation is simply not part of the standard output interface.

Forward hooks solve this cleanly. They provide a general, non-invasive mechanism to capture \emph{any} intermediate tensor—including the SwiGLU gated activation during the live forward pass. No model surgery required, no custom forward methods, and no performance penalty beyond the memory cost of storing one extra tensor. That is why hooks are indispensable for the dual-signal probing architecture presented in this thesis.

\section{Research Framework}
\label{sec:research_framework}

With the motivation established in the preceding sections, we now turn to formalizing what exactly this research sets out to accomplish. This section articulates the core problem, spells out our objectives, identifies the technical hurdles we had to overcome, and summarizes what we believe to be the novel contributions of this work.

%% -------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{subsec:problem_statement}

RAG systems were supposed to fix the hallucination problem or at least reduce it substantially. By grounding generation in retrieved evidence, the hope was that language models would stick to what the documents actually say. In practice, however, retrieval is no guarantee of faithfulness. A model can still hallucinate even with a perfectly relevant passage sitting in its context window. 

 Why? Because several failure modes remain. The model might rely on its parametric memory instead of the retrieved text. It might misread a passage or partially integrate the context. It might confidently extrapolate beyond what the evidence actually supports. The result in each case is a fluent, convincing answer that happens to be factually unsupported.

The central problem we address is therefore not retrieval quality,it is the \textbf{absence of any inference-time feedback mechanism} connecting hallucination detection to corrective action. Current systems can retrieve good passages and generate polished answers, but without some internal reliability signal, they have no way to decide whether to trust what they just generated, try again, seek better evidence, or simply refuse to answer.

We frame this as a control problem: \emph{internal activation signals are used to estimate hallucination risk, and that estimate is fed back into the RAG pipeline to govern the final response}. Detection without action is incomplete; our goal is to close the loop.

%% -------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Research Objective}
\label{subsec:research_objective}

Given this framing, our objective is to design and evaluate a closed-loop RAG system capable of the following:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Extracting internal activation features} from transformer decoder layers while the model is actively generating a response—not after the fact, but during inference.
    \item \emph{Producing a calibrated hallucination-risk probability} by combining complementary signals from the residual stream (CEV) and the feed-forward intermediate (IAV).
\item \emph{Acting on that probability} with a controller that chooses whether to deliver a candidate response, re-generate it with the same context, support it with new retrieval, or withheld altogether.


 \item \emph{Showing measurable risk reduction}—demonstrate that the closed loop controller actually reduces the hallucination risk of delivered responses over vanilla single-pass RAG.
 
    \item \emph{Generalizing across backbones}—testing the same framework on multiple decoder-only transformers without altering their architectures or retraining their weights.
\end{enumerate}

%% -------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Technical Challenges}
\label{subsec:technical_challenges}

Building such a system is not straightforward. Three specific challenges had to be addressed.

\textbf{The detection–action disconnect.} In most existing work, hallucination detection and hallucination mitigation live in separate pipelines. A detector might flag a response as risky, but that flag does not trigger any automatic corrective behavior. We needed to wire the detection signal directly into an inference-time controller—making the two inseparable parts of a single system.

\textbf{Inference-time efficiency.} Whatever signal we use for detection has to be cheap enough to compute on every generated response. Methods that require multiple sampled generations or a separate judge model are too expensive for real-time closed-loop control. Our approach uses internal activation features that are already being computed anyway—we just observe them through hooks.

\textbf{Capturing multiple hallucination mechanisms.} Hallucination is not a single phenomenon. Sometimes the model fails to attend properly to the retrieved context (a context-integration failure). Other times it confidently injects something from its parametric memory that contradicts the evidence (a parametric-memory intrusion). A single activation signal might catch one type but miss the other. That is why we combine both CEV (residual-stream) and IAV (FFN intermediate) signals they look at different parts of the computation and capture different failure modes.

\subsection{Novelty and Contributions}
\label{subsec:contributions}

We claim five main contributions, each addressing a gap in the existing literature.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~1 - Dual-signal internal-state probing.} Rather than relying on a single hidden-state source, the framework extracts two complementary signals: CEV features from the decoder residual stream and IAV features from the SwiGLU feed-forward intermediate. The underlying hypothesis is that hallucination can stem from either weak context integration or parametric-memory intrusion—and catching both requires looking at both.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~2 — Inference-time closed-loop control.} Detection alone is not enough. The calibrated probe probability feeds directly into a controller that makes real decisions: accept, regenerate, re-retrieve, or abstain. This transforms hallucination detection from a post-hoc annotation exercise into an operational safety mechanism.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~3 — Multi-backbone evaluation.} We evaluate the identical framework on three different decoder-only transformer backbones—Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct, and Qwen3-8B—providing evidence about whether the CEV/IAV probing strategy is specific to one model or generalizes across the SwiGLU decoder family.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~4 — Transparent evaluation protocol.} We are explicit about what is a validation-split result and what is a comparison against published baselines. The evaluation reports probe performance, controller behavior, and out-of-domain transfer separately, avoiding the conflation that sometimes obscures what was actually measured.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~5 — A parameter-efficient single mid-layer probe.} Our layer-wise ablation (Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection}) demonstrates that reading from just one mid-depth layer ($L/2$) matches or outperforms the originally deployed three-layer concatenation on every backbone—while using roughly a third of the probe parameters. Less is genuinely more here.

%% -------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Evaluation Scope}
\label{subsec:evaluation_scope}

We evaluate the framework at two distinct levels, each answering a different question.

\emph{First, as a detector:} Can the CEV and IAV probes reliably rank responses by hallucination risk? We assess this on a stratified RAGTruth validation split, reporting individual and fused AUROC values. The decoder depth feeding the probes is itself selected empirically through the layer-wise ablation (Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection}), not assumed a priori.

\emph{Second, as a controller:} Does the fused risk estimate, when fed into the closed-loop policy, actually reduce the hallucination risk of responses that get delivered to the user? We measure this by tracking how often the controller accepts, regenerates, re-retrieves, or abstains—and comparing the delivered-proxy hallucination rate against vanilla RAG.

All numerical results—backbone-specific AUROC values, controller-branch statistics, closed-loop reduction percentages—appear in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.
