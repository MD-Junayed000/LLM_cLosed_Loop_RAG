\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature_review}

There exists a substantial amount of work on hallucination detection and mitigation in language models, but these two concerns are seldom addressed in a single unified system. This chapter reviews the literature relevant to each method family. We first discuss the phenomenon of hallucination itself (Section~\ref{sec:lit_hallucination}), before covering black-box and reference-based detectors (Section~\ref{sec:lit_blackbox}), internal-state probing approaches (Section~\ref{sec:lit_probing}), mechanistic work on FFN layers which motivates our IAV signal (Section~\ref{sec:lit_ffn}), closed-loop and self-corrective systems (Section~\ref{sec:lit_closedloop}), and finally cross-architecture generalization studies (Section~\ref{sec:lit_crossarch}). The chapter closes with a Background section (Section~\ref{sec:background}) that establishes the technical foundations—the transformer decoder architecture and hook capture points, the CEV and IAV signals, and the theoretical motivation for why activations can be useful for hallucination detection.

\section{Hallucination in Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{sec:lit_hallucination}

The term ``hallucination'' in the context of language models refers to output that sounds fluent and confident but is factually unsupported, unverifiable, or outright contradictory to the provided source material. It is not a niche problem, having been identified as one of the most pressing reliability issues for modern LLMs in multiple survey papers~\cite{ji2023hallucination,huang2023survey,rawte2023survey}. In RAG especially, entity-based knowledge conflicts between evidence retrieved and parametric memory can lead to hallucination even when the retriever provides relevant passages~\cite{longpre2021entity}.

The taxonomy that is repeated across these surveys makes a useful distinction. \emph{Intrinsic} hallucination is when the generated text directly contradicts the given source, the model says one thing, but the evidence says another. \emph{Extrinsic} hallucination is more subtle: the model adds claims that are plausible and might even be true, but that go beyond what the available evidence actually supports. Both types are dangerous in a RAG setting, as the user usually assumes that a retrieval-grounded system is trustworthy.

\section{Black-Box, Statistical, and Reference-Based Detection}
\label{sec:lit_blackbox}

A natural way to catch hallucinations is to regard the model as a black box and check for discrepancies in the output. One example of such approach is SelfCheckGPT~\cite{manakul2023selfcheckgpt} which creates several replies for the same question and checks the consistency of the factual information across samples. The appeal is clear: you don't need access to model weights or internal states. But here is the catch. If the model is consistently incorrect with high confidence, then all sampled replies will have the same fabrication and the approach will fail to detect it. Additionally, the inference cost is paid for by generating numerous responses for each query, which is not applicable for real-time applications.

Another one is reference-based approaches. Rather than sampling many times, they compare the created text directly with the retrieved evidence. For example, RefChecker~\cite{hu2024refchecker} decomposes the model output into fine-grained claim triplets, then validates each triplet with the reference documents. This can be quite successful for post hoc verification - but requires a separate verification pipeline, increases latency and does not leverage the generating model's own internal computation as an intervention signal.

These groups of approaches together give helpful baselines and major conceptual contributions. But none of them is completely suited for the context we analyze here. They either need frequent sampling (costly), or work \emph{after} generation (late for closed-loop control), or rely on external checkers (complexity and latency). What we want is a signal from the producing model itself, something cheap, real time and actionable.

\section{Internal-State Probing for Hallucination Detection}
\label{sec:lit_probing}

The concept behind internal-state probing is simple: if a transformer is generating hallucinated text, perhaps its deep representations already "know" something is amiss, even if the surface text looks fine. This notion has been pursued by several groups of researchers.

Azaria and Mitchell~\cite{azaria2023saplma} were amongst the first to convincingly show this.  They trained simple classifiers on the hidden activations of language models, and demonstrated that these classifiers could discriminate true from incorrect claims at about \textbf{71--83\% accuracy} depending on the base model. The inference is obvious: internal representations contain information about veracity not completely represented in output probabilities. But their SAPLMA method is a pure detector, producing a score but not doing anything with it. That score does not feed back into any corrective action.

INSIDE (also called EigenScore) extends the internal-state idea by examining the eigenspectrum of hidden representations over multiple sampled outputs~\cite{chen2024inside}. Surface-level metrics miss uncertainty that can be revealed by the covariance structure of these representations. That's a good method, but it shares the same weakness as SelfCheckGPT: it still needs multiple generations which is expensive for online use.

MIND~\cite{su2024mind} is most related to our work, which performs \emph{real-time} hallucination detection based on internal states without manual annotation for training. This supports the more general claim that internal representations can be useful at live inference, not just at post-hoc analysis. However, as most detection-based methods, MIND does not employ the multi-action controller we develop here.

A consistent finding in the literature is that useful signals for hallucination detection can be found in the internal activations. Hidden states, attention patterns, FFN activity, uncertainty measures, all of them have provided useful information. But the missing piece is the connection to action. Detection of hallucination is useful, but it becomes \emph{practically} useful only if detection causes some change in system behavior. That's the gap we're trying to close.

\section{FFN Layers as Factual Memory and Motivation for IAV Probing}
\label{sec:lit_ffn}

Why look inside the feed-forward network specifically? The motivation comes from a fascinating line of mechanistic interpretability research.

According to Geva et al.~\cite{geva2021ffn}, Transformer FFN layers are similar to key-value memories. The " keys " are textual patterns that trigger the input . The " values " are the contributions that are written back into the residual stream , which determines what tokens the model will output next . They also noticed a depth gradient with lower layers encoding surface patterns and upper layers encoding more semantic and factual information. Further empirical work has examined how these stored associations can be updated and modified~\cite{qiu2024updating}.

This has a direct consequence on our work. If FFN layers store and express factual associations, then the intermediate activation \emph{inside} the FFN, before the down-projection collapses it back into the residual stream, should contain a particularly rich signal about what parametric knowledge the model is injecting. This is exactly what our IAV (Intermediate Activation Vector) captures.

This gives us two complementary views: the residual-stream signal (CEV) and the FFN-interior signal (IAV). CEV discusses context integration, how well the model has integrated the retrieved evidence with the query and the text generated so far. IAV tells us about parametric injection - what the feed-forward network is contributing from its stored knowledge. Hallucination is possible if these two signals are not in agreement or if the IAV displays abnormal patterns.

\section{Closed-Loop and Self-Corrective Generation}
\label{sec:lit_closedloop}

The idea of making RAG systems self-corrective is not entirely new, though existing approaches differ substantially from ours in mechanism and scope.

Self-RAG~\cite{asai2024selfrag} trains a language model to generate special ``reflection tokens'' that critique its own output and decide whether additional retrieval is needed. The model learns to retrieve, generate, and self-evaluate all within a single framework. It is an elegant design, but it requires training the model with reflection-token supervision, which means modifying the backbone. Our approach, by contrast, works with completely frozen models and adds the control layer externally.

CRAG~\cite{yan2024crag} takes a different path. It adds a lightweight evaluator that checks if the obtained documents are good enough to answer the query. If not, it triggers corrective retrieval, possibly even a web search. CRAG explicitly handles the "bad retrieval" failure mode, and does so well. But its intervention signal is about \emph{quality of retrieval}, not about what the generator is actually doing internally. "Our system watches the generator itself.

Abstention is another crucial piece of the jigsaw, the ability to just say ``I don't know.'' Wen et al. \cite{wen2024abstention} survey this capacity in LLMs and argue that knowing when to reject is a safety strategy in itself. We agree, and ABSTAIN is one of four actions open to our controller.

To our knowledge, no previous work has integrated all of the following in a single system, namely, hallucination detection from the generating model's own dual CEV/IAV activations, a multi-action inference-time controller (accept/regenerate/re-retrieve/abstain), and operation on entirely frozen backbones. All the individual requisites are in the literature;  the combination and the closed-loop integration are what we contribute.

\section{Cross-Architecture Generalization}
\label{sec:lit_crossarch}

A concern with activation-probing methods is whether they generalise. Does the method transfer to a different model if we train a probe on one model's hidden states? Or are the learned patterns so specific to the architecture that we have to start over each time?

The evidence is mixed. HalluRAG~\cite{frohling2024hallurag} studied internal-state hallucination detection in a closed-domain RAG setting and found that MLP classifiers could reach up to \textbf{75\% test accuracy} but with limited generalizability across different experimental configurations. This is not surprising given how different models produce very different activation distributions, but it does underscore the need to test on more than one backbone.

CLAP (Cross-Layer Attention Probing)~\cite{zhao2024clap} goes further by evaluating fine-grained hallucination detection across \textbf{five LLMs and three task types}. Their results confirm that activation-based signals do transfer to some degree, but the strength and reliability of that transfer varies considerably across models.

Our evaluation is designed with this concern front and center. We test the identical CEV/IAV probing framework and controller design on three decoder-only transformers—Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct, and Qwen3-8B which differ in their tokenizers, training data, layer counts, and FFN intermediate dimensions. The framework remains the same, i.e., the probe weights and thresholds are calibrated per backbone. This gives us a controlled reusability test: the architecture is shared, but each deployment requires backbone-specific training. This is an honest and practical characterization of the state of internal state probing at current.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

Before we dive into the system architecture we need to cover the technical foundations that make our approach viable. This section goes through the transformer decoder architecture and where exactly we tap into it (Section~\ref{sec:decoder_arch}), formally defines the two activation signals we rely on (Section~\ref{sec:cev_iav_signals}), and explains the theoretical motivation for why those signals might be useful (Section~\ref{sec:why_activations}).

%% -------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Transformer Decoder Architecture and Hook Capture Points}
\label{sec:decoder_arch}

The three models we consider, LLaMA, Mistral and Qwen, all belong to the same broad family of decoder-only transformers. They process a token sequence $x_{1:t}$ by passing it through $L$ stacked decoder blocks. Each block follows the now-standard pre-normalization recipe. Written compactly, decoder block $\ell$ does two things:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_\ell = \mathbf{h}_{\ell-1} + \text{MHA}\bigl(\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{h}_{\ell-1})\bigr)
    \label{eq:mha}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)} = \mathbf{h}_\ell + \text{FFN}\bigl(\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{h}_\ell)\bigr)
    \label{eq:ffn_residual}
\end{equation}

\noindent First it runs multi-head attention and adds the result back to the residual stream, then it runs the feed-forward network and adds \emph{that} result back too. The final output of the block, $\mathbf{h}_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)}$, is what we call the \textbf{Contextualized Embedding Vector (CEV)}. Think of it as a snapshot of everything the model ``knows'' at layer $\ell$—the accumulated residual-stream state after both attention and FFN have had their say.

Now, inside that FFN, something interesting happens. These models use a gated architecture called SwiGLU:

\begin{equation}
    \text{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) = W_{\text{down}} \Bigl( \text{SiLU}(W_{\text{gate}}\,\mathbf{x}) \odot (W_{\text{up}}\,\mathbf{x}) \Bigr)
    \label{eq:swiglu}
\end{equation}

\noindent The $\odot$ here is the element-wise (Hadamard) product—the gate and the up-projection multiply together before the down-projection squeezes the result back into the residual-stream dimension. That bracketed gated term, just before $W_{\text{down}}$ compresses it:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_{\text{IAV}}^{(\ell)} = \text{SiLU}(W_{\text{gate}}\,\mathbf{x}) \odot (W_{\text{up}}\,\mathbf{x})
    \label{eq:iav_def}
\end{equation}

\noindent \emph{That} is our \textbf{Intermediate Activation Vector (IAV)}. We grab it right before the down-projection writes the FFN's contribution back into the residual stream. It lives in a much higher-dimensional space than the residual stream itself (14,336 dimensions for Mistral and LLaMA, 12,288 for Qwen), and it represents what the feed-forward network is ``about to inject'' into the computation.

Figure~\ref{fig:decoder_block} shows these two capture points schematically within a single decoder block.

\end{figure}

\subsection{CEV and IAV Signals}
\label{sec:cev_iav_signals}

So we have two signals, and they capture genuinely different things. Let us be precise about what each one tells us.

The \textbf{CEV} is the residual-stream state after a decoder block has finished its work. By the time we reach a mid-depth layer, this vector encodes not just token identity but how the model has blended the retrieved context with the query and with whatever text it has generated so far. If the model is integrating the evidence well, the CEV should reflect that. If it is ignoring the context or mixing it with contradictory parametric knowledge, the CEV may show signs of that too.

The \textbf{IAV} is something different entirely. It reflects what the feed-forward network is contributing the parametric signal that will be added to the residual stream. This is theoretically motivated by work showing that FFN layers behave like key-value memories, storing factual associations that get expressed during generation~\cite{geva2021ffn,meng2022locating}. If the model is ``remembering'' something from training and injecting it into the output—regardless of whether the retrieved context supports it—the IAV is where that signal lives.

In practice, we extract both signals from a single mid-depth layer (approximately $L/2$), take the last-token activation, and pass the resulting vectors to lightweight MLP probes. The probes' calibrated outputs are then fused into a single hallucination-risk score. Why a single mid-depth layer? The layer-wise ablation in Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection} provides the empirical justification.

\subsection{Why Activations Can Be Useful for Hallucination Detection}
\label{sec:why_activations}

At this point a skeptical reader might ask: why should internal activations tell us anything about hallucination that the output text doesn't? The motivation is inspired by two observations in previous work.

\textbf{The residual stream encodes more than just token identity.} The residual stream in the middle and later layers of a transformer has accumulated not just what tokens appeared in the input but \emph{how} the model integrated and processed those tokens. Simple classifiers trained on these hidden states have been shown to predict factuality and truthfulness with non-trivial accuracy in prior work~\cite{azaria2023saplma}. The surface text might look fine, but the internal representations might show uncertainty or inconsistency. \textbf{FFN layers are not generic nonlinearities.} Mechanistic interpretability work has found that transformer FFN layers work somewhat like key-value memories \cite{geva2021ffn}. Stored associations are triggered by input patterns and those associations influence what the model generates next. Factual knowledge can be localized and even modified within specific FFN modules~\cite{meng2022locating}. This suggests that the IAV signal, the intermediate activation before downprojection, might encode a very direct signature of what parametric knowledge the model is injecting.

We do not claim that either one of the signals is a perfect hallucination detector in isolation. A less ambitious hypothesis is that CEV and IAV provide \emph{complementary evidence} . CEV measures the degree of the model's incorporation of the retrieved context. IAV represents the parametric memory contribution of the feed-forward network. The more signals agree the more certain we are that a response appears to be grounded. If one or both flags are raised, the controller can intervene.

\subsection{Illustrative CEV/IAV Examples}
\label{sec:illustrative_examples}

We illustrate these ideas with three example scenarios. These are just illustrative, the activation values shown are schematic and truncated, not literal neuron readouts. The point is to show \emph{how} the probe interprets various activation patterns, including a failure case.

\subsubsection{Grounded Response}

\begin{groundedbox}[$\checkmark$ Example 2.7.1: Grounded Response --- ACCEPT]
\textbf{Query:} ``What year did the Treaty of Westphalia end the Thirty Years' War?''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Retrieved context:} ``The Peace of Westphalia was signed in 1648 and ended the Thirty Years' War.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Generated response:} ``The Treaty of Westphalia ended the Thirty Years' War in 1648.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected label:} Grounded / non-hallucinated.
\end{groundedbox}

\begin{activationbox}[Illustrative Activation Pattern]
\textit{CEV at layer~16 (4096-dim, truncated):}\\
$\uparrow$ High positive values in ``factual recall'' dimensions\\
$\uparrow$ Pattern consistent with context-supported generation\\[6pt]
\textit{IAV at layer~16 (14336-dim, pre-down\_proj, truncated):}\\
$\uparrow$ Strongly activated ``historical date'' neurons\\
$\uparrow$ MLP injecting a well-encoded factual year---aligned with context
\end{activationbox}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent Here the retrieved passage directly supports the generated answer. Both CEV and IAV show patterns consistent with faithful, context-grounded generation. The fused probe score comes out low:

\noindent\textbf{Controller decision:} $p_{\text{hallucination}} = 0.33 < \theta_{\text{hallu}} = 0.62 \;\Rightarrow\;$ \textcolor{acceptgreen}{\textbf{ACCEPT}}.

\subsubsection{Hallucinated Response}

\begin{hallucinatedbox}[$\boldsymbol{\times}$ Example 2.7.2: Hallucinated Response --- ABSTAIN]
\textbf{Query:} ``What is the official 2024 census population of the lost city of Atlantis?''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Retrieved context:} ``A 2024 census of South Atlantic territories found populations across several islands.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Generated response:} ``According to the 2024 census, Atlantis had a population of 140,634.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected label:} Hallucinated / unsupported.
\end{hallucinatedbox}

\begin{activationbox}[Illustrative Activation Pattern]
$\uparrow$ Weak, mixed CEV activation---no strong factual grounding signal\\
$\uparrow$ Context was only superficially related; residual stream shows low coherence\\
$\uparrow$ ``Census'' and ``number'' neurons partially fire in IAV\\
$\uparrow$ But ``Atlantis'' + ``population'' triggers confabulation patterns\\
$\uparrow$ IAV encodes something like ``fabricating a plausible number from context cues''
\end{activationbox}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent The retrieved passage mentions a census and population figures---but for real places, not Atlantis. The model latches onto superficial pattern matches and confidently invents a specific number. Both the CEV (low coherence with context) and IAV (confabulation patterns) produce high risk scores:

\noindent\textbf{Controller decision:} $p_{\text{hallucination}} = 0.78 \geq \theta_{\text{abs}} = 0.75 \;\Rightarrow\;$ \textcolor{abstainred}{\textbf{ABSTAIN}}.

\subsubsection{Failure Case: Plausible but Unsupported Extraction}

\begin{failurebox}[$\triangle$ Example 2.7.3: Failure Case --- False ACCEPT]
\textbf{Query:} ``What did I have for breakfast yesterday?''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Retrieved context:} ``Common breakfast foods include eggs, toast, coffee, and fruit.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Generated response:} ``Based on the context, you had eggs, toast, and coffee.''\\[4pt]
\textbf{Expected label:} Hallucinated / unsupported.\\[4pt]
\textbf{Why this is tricky:} The passage is topically related but lacks the specific personal fact. The model treats generic context as if it were supporting evidence.
\end{failurebox}

\begin{activationbox}[Illustrative Activation Pattern]
$\uparrow$ \textbf{Nearly identical} to the factual grounding pattern!\\
$\uparrow$ Model found relevant context (``breakfast foods'') and extracted an answer\\
$\uparrow$ Hidden state looks like ordinary factual retrieval\\
$\uparrow$ ``Food'' + ``list'' neurons strongly activated in IAV\\
$\uparrow$ MLP injecting a food list---indistinguishable from answering a real factual question
\end{activationbox}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent This is where the probe fails. The model \emph{is} doing something that looks exactly like grounded extraction---it found food items in the context and reported them. The problem is that ``common breakfast foods'' is not the same as ``what \emph{you} ate yesterday.'' But from the model's internal perspective, the computation is virtually identical to legitimate context-supported generation. The probe scores it as low-risk:

\noindent\textbf{Controller decision:} $p_{\text{hallucination}} = 0.42 < \theta_{\text{hallu}} = 0.62 \;\Rightarrow\;$ \textcolor{abstainred}{\textbf{ACCEPT} $\boldsymbol{\times}$ (WRONG)}.

\vspace{0.5em}
We include this failure case deliberately. It illustrates an important limitation: our probe is not a perfect factuality oracle. It reads activation patterns, and when the pattern of ``plausible extraction from topically relevant context'' looks identical to ``genuine grounded retrieval,'' the probe cannot distinguish them. Recognizing this boundary is essential for understanding what the system can and cannot do.
