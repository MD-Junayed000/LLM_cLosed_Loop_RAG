\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:problem_statement}

Although RAG systems improve factual grounding by conditioning generation on retrieved evidence, they do not automatically guarantee that the generated answer is faithful to that evidence. A candidate response may still contain unsupported or contradictory information because the model can rely on parametric memory, misread the retrieved passage, or integrate the context only partially.

The central problem addressed in this work is therefore not retrieval alone, but the \textbf{absence of an inference-time feedback mechanism} that links hallucination detection to corrective action. Existing systems may retrieve relevant passages and generate fluent answers, but without an internal reliability signal, they cannot decide whether to accept, retry, re-retrieve, or abstain before delivering the response.

This study frames \emph{hallucination mitigation as a closed-loop control problem: internal activation signals are used to estimate hallucination risk, and that estimate is fed back into the RAG pipeline to control the final response}.

%% -------------------------------------------------
\section{Research Objective}
\label{sec:research_objective}

The objective of this study is to design and evaluate a closed-loop RAG system that can:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Extract internal activation features from transformer decoder layers during inference.}
    \item \emph{Estimate a calibrated hallucination-risk probability from complementary residual-stream and FFN activation signals.}
    \item \emph{Use this probability to decide whether a candidate response should be accepted, regenerated, supported by re-retrieval, or withheld.}
    \item \emph{Evaluate whether the closed-loop controller reduces delivered hallucination risk while preserving answer utility.}
    \item \emph{Test the framework across multiple decoder-only transformer backbones without modifying their core architectures.}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Technical Challenges}
\label{sec:technical_challenges}

The proposed framework addresses three technical challenges.

\textbf{First}, hallucination detection and mitigation are usually treated as separate stages. A detector may assign a risk score to a generated response, but that score is often not connected to an automatic correction policy. This work links the detection signal directly to an inference-time controller.

\textbf{Second}, the detection signal must be lightweight enough to support inference-time use. Instead of relying on multiple sampled generations or an external judge model, the proposed method uses internal activation features from the generating model itself.

\textbf{Third}, the internal signal must capture more than one hallucination mechanism. Residual-stream features can reflect context integration, while FFN intermediate activations can reflect parametric-memory contributions. The framework therefore combines both CEV and IAV signals and evaluates whether their fusion improves hallucination-risk estimation.

\section{Novelty and Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

This work makes five main contributions.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~1 --- Dual-signal internal-state probing.} The framework combines two complementary activation sources: CEV features from the decoder residual stream and IAV features from the SwiGLU FFN intermediate activation. This design is motivated by the hypothesis that hallucination can arise both from weak context integration and from parametric-memory intrusion.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~2 --- Inference-time closed-loop control.} Instead of using hallucination detection only as a post-hoc score, the calibrated probe probability is used by a controller that determines whether the response should be accepted, regenerated, supported by re-retrieval, or abstained.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~3 --- Multi-backbone evaluation.} The same framework is evaluated across Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct, and Qwen3-8B, allowing the study to examine whether the CEV/IAV probing strategy transfers across multiple decoder-only transformer families.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~4 --- Reproducible evaluation protocol.} The study reports validation-set probe performance, closed-loop controller behavior, and out-of-domain transfer behavior, while explicitly separating validation-split results from comparisons against published test-set baselines.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution~5 --- A parameter-efficient single mid-layer probe.} A controlled layer-wise ablation (Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection}) shows that reading the probe from a single mid-depth layer ($L/2$) matches or beats the originally deployed three-layer concatenation on every backbone while using about one-third of the probe parameters, giving a system-agnostic and cheaper detector.

\section{Evaluation Scope}
\label{sec:evaluation_scope}

The framework is evaluated at two levels.

\emph{First}, the CEV and IAV probes are assessed as hallucination-risk detectors using a stratified RAGTruth validation split. Their individual and fused AUROC values are compared to determine whether dual-signal probing improves over either signal alone. The decoder depth that feeds the probes is itself selected empirically through a layer-wise ablation (Section~\ref{sec:layer_selection}) rather than assumed.

\emph{Second}, the fused hallucination-risk estimate is used inside a closed-loop RAG controller. The controller is evaluated by observing how often it accepts, regenerates, re-retrieves, or abstains, and whether these decisions reduce the hallucination risk of delivered responses.

All numerical results, including backbone-specific AUROC values and controller-branch statistics, are reported in the Results chapter (Chapter~\ref{ch:results}).
